Cox's Bazar

History & Origins

From a Rakhine fishing settlement to Bangladesh's tourism capital — the story behind the name, and the coast that became Asia's longest beach.

1798

How Cox's Bazar Got Its Name

In 1798, Captain Hiram Cox, an officer of the British East India Company, was posted to oversee the resettlement of Arakanese refugees fleeing conflict in neighbouring Burma, at an outpost then known as Palongkee. After his death the following year, a market was established here in his memory — Cox's Bazar, literally "Cox's Market."

Older names for the area include Panowa, meaning "yellow flower" — a reminder that this coastline carried identities long before the name most visitors know it by today.

🏛️ British East India Company ⛵ Palongkee Outpost 🌼 Panowa — "Yellow Flower"

A Thousand Years in the Making

From Fishing Village to Beach Capital

The coast has been settled for over a thousand years, long before Cox arrived — the Rakhine community trace their presence here back to the 9th century. For most of its history since, Cox's Bazar remained a quiet fishing port. Its transformation into Bangladesh's primary beach destination is a 20th- and 21st-century story, driven first by domestic tourism and, more recently, by direct flights from Dhaka and growing international interest in its coastline, islands, and Rakhine heritage.

Right Now

Cox's Bazar Today

Cox's Bazar is now Bangladesh's busiest coastal tourism hub, anchored by the beach zones, Marine Drive, and the islands covered throughout this guide. It stands today as Asia's longest natural sea beach — one of the longest unbroken stretches of sand anywhere on Earth — a distinction that placed it among the official finalist candidates in the New7Wonders of Nature global campaign, alongside Komodo Island and the Amazon Rainforest.
