When to Visit

Choose your season

November — February

Peak Season

The best time to visit — cool and dry with temperatures of 18–25°C, calm seas, and maximum visibility. Saint Martin's Island ships run through January 31st, Cox's Bazar departure only, under a daily visitor cap. The beach is at its most beautiful and most crowded. Book accommodation well in advance, especially during public holidays.

March — May

Shoulder Season

Warm and significantly less crowded than peak — temperatures rise to 28–32°C. Good beach conditions through April. By May the humidity builds and the first pre-monsoon storms begin, marking the start of the region's cyclone season — worth checking the forecast in the days before a May visit. The island is closed to tourism from February through October to protect its coral reef. Prices are lower and the experience is more local.

June — September

Monsoon Season

Heavy rain, rough seas, and almost no tourists — but the hills are intensely green, the waterfalls at Himchari are at full force, and prices drop 40–60%. Swimming is not advisable. Island boats are suspended. For landscape photography and solitude, this is actually the most remarkable time to visit.

October

Transition

The overlooked sweet spot — the rains ease, the landscape stays green from the monsoon, the seas begin to calm, and the crowds haven't arrived yet. October sits at the tail end of the region's cyclone season, so it's worth checking the forecast in the lead-up to a visit. Boat services resume November 1st under the new regulated season. Prices are still low. Sunsets are extraordinary. If flexibility allows, October is one of the most rewarding months to visit.

Arriving From Abroad

Getting Here, Wherever You're Coming From

International visitors arrive through Hazrat Shahjalal International Airport in Dhaka, well connected to major hubs across the Middle East, South and Southeast Asia, and Europe. From there, reaching Cox's Bazar is part of the everyday rhythm of travel here — a quick domestic flight, a scenic train ride, or an overnight bus, each with its own character.

Getting There

From Dhaka to Cox's Bazar

By Air

The fastest option — Dhaka (DAC) to Cox's Bazar (CXB) takes just 1 hour, with Biman Bangladesh, US-Bangla, and Novoair operating multiple flights daily.

⏱ 1 hour💰 BDT 3,500–8,000

By Bus

Direct AC buses from Dhaka's Sayedabad terminal — depart evening, arrive morning. Shyamoli, Hanif, and S Alam are the main operators. Comfortable enough for an overnight journey.

⏱ 10–12 hours💰 BDT 700–1,400

By Train + Bus

Train from Dhaka to Chittagong (7 hrs), then bus or shared car from Chittagong to Cox's Bazar (3 hrs). Longer but scenic — the train through the countryside is worth doing once.

⏱ 10–11 hours💰 BDT 500–1,200

Getting Around

Local transport

CNG Auto-Rickshaw

The main way to move around — negotiate a price before you get in. Marine Drive end-to-end takes about 3 hours and costs BDT 800–1,200 negotiated.

Speed Boat

For Moheshkhali and Sonadia — speed boats from the main jetty. Shared boats run on demand; private hire is negotiable. Always agree price before boarding.

Ship to Saint Martin's

Tourist ships depart from Cox's Bazar — the Teknaf and Inani routes are currently closed. The season runs November 1 – January 31, with a daily visitor cap and advance booking required.

Motorbike Hire

Available for hire near Kolatoli — BDT 400–600 per day. The most flexible way to explore Marine Drive at your own pace. International licence recommended.

Know Before You Go

Essential information

🛂 Visa

Most visitors can obtain a Visa on Arrival at Dhaka airport — valid 30 days. Some nationalities must apply in advance. Requirements vary by country — check with your nearest Bangladesh Mission or visit the official visa portal.

💵 Currency

Bangladeshi Taka (BDT). Currency counters at Dhaka airport and banks in Cox's Bazar town offer reliable exchange rates; hotels typically exchange at a small markup for convenience. Major hotels accept cards — smaller restaurants and markets are cash only.

📱 Connectivity

Grameenphone and Robi are the leading local providers — pick up a SIM at the airport with your passport, or from any shop in town.

🏥 Health

No vaccinations specifically required. Standard precautions — drink bottled water, be cautious with street food initially. Cox's Bazar District Hospital is the main facility; Dhaka is the nearest for serious care.

⚡ Power

Bangladesh uses 220V / 50Hz. Type C and Type D plug sockets. Power cuts are occasional — better hotels have generators. Bring a power bank for day trips to the islands.

🕌 Ramadan & Prayer Times

During Ramadan, many local restaurants adjust hours or close during daytime fasting; hotel restaurants generally stay open for guests. Friday midday prayers may briefly affect local market hours.

📞 Emergency Contacts

National emergency line: 999 (police, fire, ambulance). Cox's Bazar District Hospital handles urgent medical care. Your embassy or consulate in Dhaka is the right first call for lost passports or other serious matters abroad.

🕐 Time & Week

Bangladesh runs on Bangladesh Standard Time (UTC+6), with no daylight saving. The weekend here is Friday–Saturday rather than Saturday–Sunday — useful to know when planning around bank or office hours.

🛸 Drones

Drone use in Bangladesh requires prior permission from civil aviation authorities. If aerial photography is part of your trip, arrange clearance well before you travel.

Quick Answers

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Cox's Bazar safe for tourists?

Yes — the core beach and town areas (Laboni, Kolatoli, Sugandha, Inani) are calm, well-trodden, and used to tourists. If your plans extend further south toward Teknaf, it's worth checking current local conditions before you go.

How many days do I need?

Three days covers the beach, Marine Drive, and one nearby destination like Himchari or a Rakhine village. Five days lets you add Saint Martin's or Moheshkhali without rushing.

Is alcohol available?

Alcohol is served by a number of licensed hotels and restaurants.

Do I need a local guide?

Not for the main beach and town — it's easy to navigate independently. A guide is useful for the Himchari trail or arranging island boat trips, and most hotels can arrange one.

Which beach area is best for swimming?

Laboni and Kolatoli have flagged, supervised swimming zones. The Bay of Bengal has strong currents elsewhere, so stick to flagged areas and avoid swimming alone at low tide.

Is it good for families or solo travellers?

Yes to both — Kolatoli/Sugandha is the most convenient base for families, and the area is generally comfortable for solo travellers, including women.

Can I drink the tap water?

No — stick to bottled water, which is cheap and widely available, including at most hotels.

What should I wear?

Many visitors choose breathable, light clothing for the heat, wearing swimwear on the beach itself and a cover-up when moving into town or markets.

Is it okay to take photos of local people?

Most visitors ask before photographing people directly, especially in villages, markets, or at religious sites.

Is tipping expected?

Tipping isn't required, though many visitors leave a small amount for hotel staff, guides, and drivers.

Can I negotiate prices?

Negotiating a price beforehand is common practice for CNG rides, boat hire, and souvenirs.

Can I use foreign currency like USD or EUR?

Bangladeshi Taka is the currency accepted directly at most businesses. Foreign currency such as USD or EUR can be exchanged at the airport, banks, or hotels.

Should I get a local SIM or use roaming?

Local SIM cards (Grameenphone, Robi) are inexpensive and offer the best coverage along the coast. International roaming is also an option for shorter stays.

Should I travel independently or book a package tour?

Both approaches are common. Independent travel suits the main beach and town areas well, while package tours offer a convenient single booking for itineraries that include the islands or culture circuit.

Are there any useful Bangla phrases to know?

A few that help along the way: ধন্যবাদ (dhonnobad) — thank you; কত? (koto) — how much?; ভালো (bhalo) — good. English is spoken to a reasonable degree in tourist-facing businesses in Cox's Bazar town.
